About

The Game of Life is a 2D cellular automaton created by John Conway in 1970. Although it's referred to as a "game", it is not a game in the traditional sense; there are no players, and the state of the game depends on solely its previous state. Each square in the grid is referred to as a "cell", surrounded by the eight "neighbor" cells adjacent to it. Each cell is either alive (black) or dead (white). The game follows four basic rules:

Any alive cell that has fewer than two neighbors will die, as if by underpopulation.

Any alive cell that has two or three neighbors will remain alive.

Any alive cell that has more than three neighbors will die, as if by overpopulation.

Any dead cell that has exactly three neighbors will come to life, as if by reproduction.

Despite its simple rules, the game allows for complex behaviors, and the computaional power of a Universal Turing Machine. Since its creation, Conway's Game of Life as become immensely popular with computer scientists and mathematicians.
